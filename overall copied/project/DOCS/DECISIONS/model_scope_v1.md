Final modeling scope

Keep the current AHP-weighted ranking model and 26-KPI structure as the core scoring engine, but simulate only the subset of KPIs that a federal government can plausibly move over the next three years. The simulation time step should be quarterly: that is frequent enough to reflect rollout lags and milestone timing, but still simple enough for a business audience. The time horizon should be 3 years as the base case, because the course brief, the implementation cards, and the existing dashboard all align to a 1–3 year decision window.

For KPI gap reduction, the primary benchmark should be the best transferable comparator, not the absolute global frontier. In practice, use Canada’s natural peer set first, because the methodology already identifies Cluster 3—UK, Germany, France, Spain—as Canada’s most relevant comparison group; then allow a best-in-class transferable comparator outside that cluster where appropriate. Keep the US/China frontier as a stretch case in the appendix only, not as the headline target, because those ecosystems operate at a structurally different scale in the near term.

Dashboard scope

The consulting dashboard should show the median Monte Carlo outcome as the headline projection, not the mean. The median will be more credible for executives because it is less distorted by a few very optimistic runs; pair it with a simple uncertainty band such as an interquartile range. The mean can still appear in the appendix for transparency, but it should not drive the front-page story.

On the main dashboard, show only what helps a decision-maker answer, “Are these policies working?” That means: Canada’s current versus projected composite score and rank; the three policy bundles and their rollout timeline; 4–6 policy-sensitive KPIs that visibly move first; and a simple indicator of percent of benchmark gap closed. The current mock-up is already directionally right: timeline, selected KPI paths, and resulting rank. What should stay out of the consulting dashboard is the full KPI catalog, full country tables, raw simulation distributions, and methodological diagnostics. Those belong in the appendix.

Impact-attribution scope

Attribute impact at the policy-bundle level, not at the individual action level. In other words, show that the commercialization bundle primarily moves commercial score, adoption, and investment activity; the research bundle primarily moves research, development, patents, and notable models; and the talent bundle primarily moves talent, job postings, and skill migration. That will feel much more defensible than claiming that one tax credit or one immigration stream caused an exact number of composite-score points.

Also keep attribution directional and ranged, not absolute. Your current ranking model is an excellent prioritization tool, but it is still a weighted scorecard built from heterogeneous indicators rather than a pure causal system. So the dashboard should say “most likely contribution” or “primary driver,” not “proved impact.”

Missing external calibration data needed

The biggest missing calibration layer is real-world evidence on how fast policy changes convert into KPI movement. The most important gaps are sector-level Canadian AI adoption rates by industry and firm size; first-customer procurement conversion rates from pilot to multi-year contract; Series B/C funding conversion and scale-up survival data; senior AI talent inflow, retention, and outflow data; and research-to-patent / research-to-commercialization lag data. The recommendation deck also makes clear that demand-side bottlenecks are central, so procurement-cycle and enterprise-adoption data matter as much as research funding data.

You also need comparator calibration from countries whose policies are actually transferable to Canada—especially around procurement, adoption incentives, compute access, and public-sector capability—so the modeled uplift rates are grounded in observed international experience rather than judgment alone.

Risks to avoid

Do not position the model as a forecast of Canada’s exact future rank. It should be framed as a policy-informed scenario simulator. Do not overclaim fast movement in structurally slower areas like infrastructure. Do not double-count benefits where multiple policy bundles touch the same KPI. Do not use the US or China as the headline near-term target. And do not overload the dashboard with technical detail; the course brief is explicit that the audience is business-oriented and wants insight for decision-making, not a technical methods lecture.

The bottom line: quarterly simulation, 3-year horizon, transferable-comparator benchmark, median headline result, and an executive dashboard that shows only the levers, leading KPIs, rank effect, and uncertainty band.
