Use peer-cluster best as the default executive benchmark and global best as the appendix stretch benchmark. Your methodology already defines Cluster 3 (UK, Canada, Germany, France, Spain) as Canada’s natural peer group, and it explicitly says that peer-cluster benchmarking was used for the gap analysis. It also says USA/China operate at a structurally inaccessible scale in the near term, which makes them poor headline comparators for the main consulting story.

Chart recommendation

Your course brief says the presentation should be business-oriented, not overly technical, and focused on insights for decision-making. So each recommendation should get one clean headline chart with baseline, stand-alone counterfactual, and benchmark.

For Recommendation 1 — Talent retention, use a horizontal dumbbell chart with 3–5 KPIs: Canada baseline on the left, stand-alone counterfactual in the middle, peer-cluster best on the right. This works because talent is a “close the outflow gap” story, and dumbbells make that immediately visible.

For Recommendation 2 — Commercialization / scale-up, use a gap-closure bridge chart. Start with Canada’s current commercialization/development gap, then show how much of that gap the recommendation closes on the directly affected KPIs, ending at the stand-alone counterfactual. This fits the “valley of death” narrative much better than a generic bar chart because it shows movement across the commercialization pipeline. Your materials already frame commercialization and scale-up as Canada’s biggest missed opportunity.

For Recommendation 3 — First-customer AI adoption credit, use a simple adoption trajectory chart: baseline trend, no-policy continuation, and stand-alone policy path, with a peer-best target line. Your own Problem 3 brief already suggests an adoption trend versus “what we need” target, so this is the most natural executive visual.

Executive wording

Use “gap reduction” as progress toward a benchmark, not as a guaranteed causal promise.

Best consulting-style phrasing:

“This recommendation narrows Canada’s gap to the best-performing peer on the KPIs it directly influences.”
“In a stand-alone scenario, this policy closes part of the gap between Canada’s current position and peer-best performance.”
“This is a benchmark-closing estimate, not a forecast of exact rank change.”
“The recommendation moves Canada materially toward peer-frontier performance on the KPIs most tied to this policy.”

If you want a more polished slide line, use:

“Estimated gap closed vs peer-best, shown on a stand-alone basis.”
“Share of Canada’s benchmark gap addressed by this recommendation alone.”

For the appendix or speaker notes, a stronger version is:

“Against a frozen baseline, this stand-alone counterfactual shows how much of Canada’s current KPI gap could reasonably be narrowed if this recommendation were implemented on its own.”

Caveat text

Use this exact caveat on the slide or in the footnote:

Each recommendation is shown as a separate stand-alone counterfactual against the same Canada baseline. These effects are not additive. The recommendations overlap on several KPIs, interact over time, and may move some of the same outcomes through different channels. Summing the three charts would overstate total impact.

And a shorter version for a busy slide:

Stand-alone impacts only. Do not add across recommendations.

That caveat is especially important here because your recommendations overlap on commercialization, adoption, investment, talent, and spillovers across pillars. The revised report itself groups the score-uplift logic by recommendation and notes that different KPIs move first versus later, which supports treating each one as a separate scenario rather than a stackable total.
